Quality of Life Around the World

This visualisation explores global quality of life indicators. It focuses on economic and social themes. This visualisation is designed to help students, researchers or merely curious viewers better understand how various factors interact across countries. The data draws from reputable open sources such as the World Bank and OECD.

Various idioms are utilised to highlight geographic patterns and statistical corelations, making it easier for users to compare regions and uncover insights. I encourage the reader to ask themselves "In what regard is living in Australia better than some other country that is often idolised?". Interactive features such as tooltips allow for these personalised insights.

Socioeconomic Breakdown

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) per capita, adjusted for Purchasing Power Parity (PPP), gives us a fairer comparison of how much people can actually buy in different countries. Instead of just looking at raw income, PPP accounts for cost of living and price levels — making it possible to compare living standards across the globe.


Unsurprisingly, wealthy nations like Luxembourg, Switzerland, and Ireland often top the charts, with small populations and strong financial sectors giving them a boost. On the flip side, emerging economies such as India and Nigeria rank lower, but their GDP per capita has been growing steadily over the last decade.

Fun fact: If the European microstate of Luxembourg were a person, they’d be one of the richest in the world — its PPP-adjusted GDP per capita is more than 5 times the global average. Meanwhile, Qatar has historically been a frontrunner thanks to oil and gas, though diversification is shifting its numbers. Another surprise? Despite being one of the world’s largest economies, China’s per capita GDP still lags far behind developed nations, reminding us how population size plays a huge role.

Note that GDP per Capita is basically an averaged metric. This means it does not solely represent the median person - that if there are a handful of rich people this would skew the value higher.
